# Academic Writing Skill

## 1. Academic Writing Workflow

**Core principle: writing starts from the researcher's ideas, not from the model's imagination.**

- Always begin from the researcher's notes, outlines, data, or prior drafts. Never generate scholarly text ex nihilo.
- If the researcher provides only a vague direction, ask targeted questions before writing: What is the central claim? What evidence supports it? Who is the audience? What existing work must be engaged?
- Draft in stages: outline → argument skeleton → section drafts → revision. Do not skip to polished prose without a confirmed structure.
- When expanding researcher notes, stay faithful to their claims and emphasis. You may tighten, clarify, and organize — but do not introduce new arguments or substantive claims the researcher did not make.
- Flag any place where the text goes beyond what the researcher supplied: use `[inference]` or ask for confirmation.
- When the researcher says "write a literature review on X," the first step is to ask what sources they have or want covered, not to fabricate citations.

## 2. Thesis Structure Guide

### Chapter Organization

| Chapter | Purpose | Typical Length (pages) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Introduction | Establish research gap, state problem, pose questions, outline thesis | 10–20 | Funnel structure (see §8) |
| Literature Review | Map the field, identify gaps, position the study | 25–40 | Thematic or chronological; must end with a gap statement |
| Methodology | Justify and describe research design, data, and analysis | 15–30 | Must defend choices, not just describe them |
| Results | Present findings without interpretation | 20–40 | Tables, figures, descriptive statistics; raw reporting |
| Discussion | Interpret results, connect to literature, acknowledge limits | 15–30 | See discussion structure in §8 |
| Conclusion | Summarize contributions, state implications, suggest future work | 5–10 | No new evidence |

### Introduction Checklist
- [ ] Broad context: why does this area matter?
- [ ] Specific problem: what gap, contradiction, or unanswered question exists?
- [ ] Research questions or hypotheses stated explicitly
- [ ] Contribution statement: what this thesis does that prior work does not
- [ ] Thesis outline (roadmap of chapters)

### Literature Review Principles
- Organize thematically, not as an annotated list
- Every source cited must serve the argument (establish consensus, show gaps, contrast positions)
- End with an explicit gap statement: "Despite X, Y remains unaddressed…"
- Group sources by idea, not by author; avoid the "Smith (2020) found… Jones (2021) found…" pattern without synthesis

### Methodology Must Include
- Research paradigm and rationale (why this approach, not just what)
- Data collection: sources, sampling, instruments, procedures
- Analysis method and justification
- Validity/reliability/trustworthiness measures
- Ethical considerations
- Limitations acknowledged up front

## 3. Paper Structure Templates

### 3a. IMRAD (Empirical / Experimental)

```
Abstract (150–250 words, structured or unstructured)
1. Introduction
   - Context and significance
   - Literature positioning
   - Research question / hypothesis
   - Paper organization
2. Methods
   - Participants / Materials / Procedure
   - Analytical approach
   - Justification for each choice
3. Results
   - Findings organized by research question
   - Tables and figures with descriptive captions
   - No interpretation here
4. Discussion
   - Interpret each finding
   - Connect to prior literature
   - Limitations
   - Implications and future directions
5. Conclusion (sometimes merged with Discussion)
References
Appendices (if any)
```

### 3b. Argument-Driven (Theoretical / Analytical)

```
Abstract (150–250 words)
1. Introduction
   - Problem statement
   - Why existing frameworks fall short
   - Thesis statement (the argument)
2. Conceptual / Theoretical Grounding
   - Key concepts defined
   - Prior frameworks analyzed
   - Gaps or tensions identified
3. Analysis (may be multiple sections)
   - Each section advances one sub-argument
   - Evidence: textual, logical, case-based, comparative
4. Discussion
   - Synthesize sub-arguments into main claim
   - Respond to anticipated objections
   - Limitations
5. Conclusion
   - Restate contribution
   - Broader implications
References
```

### 3c. Systematic Review

```
Abstract (structured: Background, Objectives, Methods, Results, Conclusions)
1. Introduction
   - Rationale for the review
   - Review questions
2. Methods
   - Search strategy (databases, terms, date range)
   - Inclusion/exclusion criteria
   - Screening process (PRISMA flow)
   - Data extraction and synthesis method
   - Quality appraisal
3. Results
   - Search outcomes
   - Study characteristics (summary table)
   - Synthesis by theme/question
   - Quality assessment results
4. Discussion
   - Summary of findings
   - Comparison with prior reviews
   - Strengths and limitations of the review
   - Implications for practice and research
5. Conclusion
References
Appendices (search strings, full extraction tables)
```

## 4. Academic Register Principles

### Precision
- Use the most specific term available: "three-item scale" not "a brief measure"; "statistically significant at α = .01" not "highly significant"
- Define technical terms on first use; do not assume disciplinary consensus on meaning

### Hedging (Calibrated by Claim Strength)

| Claim Strength | Typical Hedge | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Strong (direct evidence) | indicates, demonstrates | "The results indicate that…" |
| Moderate (correlational) | suggests, is associated with | "This pattern suggests a relationship between…" |
| Weak (speculative) | may, could, might, appears to | "These findings may reflect…" |
| Very weak (exploratory) | it is possible that, one interpretation is | "One possible interpretation is that…" |

**Rules:**
- Never hedge a direct replication: if you replicated a known result, say so directly.
- Never claim more than the data supports: "proves" is almost always wrong; "establishes" only for deductive arguments.
- Avoid hedge stacking: "It might perhaps possibly suggest…" — pick one calibrated term.

### Formal but Not Stiff
- Prefer "we argue" over "it is argued by the authors"
- Prefer "the data show" over "it can be seen that the data show"
- Contractions are genre-dependent: avoid in formal journal articles; acceptable in some conference papers and most theses after supervisor approval
- Avoid ornamentalLatin: "e.g." and "i.e." are fine; "ibid.," "op. cit.," and "viz." add friction without clarity

### Discipline-Specific Conventions
- Check target journal/department style guide before finalizing register
- Psychology: APA hedging conventions; report statistics inline (F, t, p, d, η²)
- History: footnote-heavy, present-tense for established facts, past-tense for events
- Computer science: passive voice more accepted; algorithm pseudocode conventions
- Education: first person accepted; reflective voice common in methodology

## 5. Paragraph Construction Rules

### Length Variation
- Vary between 3 and 12 sentences per paragraph.
- A paragraph under 3 sentences is likely underdeveloped (unless it is a deliberate rhetorical punch).
- A paragraph over 12 sentences likely contains multiple ideas that should be split.

### Opening Strategy Variation
Never start consecutive paragraphs the same way. Rotate among:

1. **Topic sentence** (most common): "A second factor complicating this analysis is…"
2. **Transition from prior point**: "This tension between cost and quality recurs in…"
3. **Question or problem**: "Why, then, do organizations persist with this approach?"
4. **Concession**: "Although these results appear robust, several conditions limit…"
5. **Illustration or case**: "The 2018 reform in Country X illustrates this dynamic."
6. **Definition or conceptual move**: "Resilience, in this framework, denotes not merely recovery but adaptive reconfiguration."

### Structural Rules
- Each paragraph must advance the argument. If removing it changes nothing, delete it.
- A paragraph should not merely summarize a source; it must use the source to support a claim.
- Avoid parallel structure across paragraphs: if three consecutive paragraphs all start "X argues that… Y argues that… Z argues that…," restructure.

### Example — Before and After

Before (parallel, flat):
> Smith (2020) argues that institutional pressure drives adoption. Jones (2021) argues that resource availability mediates this effect. Chen (2022) argues that leadership style is the primary predictor.

After (varied, argumentative):
> Institutional pressure alone cannot explain the observed variation in adoption rates (Smith, 2020). Resource availability mediates the relationship: organizations with slack resources adopt faster regardless of external pressure (Jones, 2021). Yet even well-resourced organizations resist change when leadership frames the innovation as disruptive rather than enabling (Chen, 2022).

## 6. Citation Integration Patterns

### Signal Phrase (Narrative Integration)
Use when the author's name matters to the argument.
> As Swales (1990) observes, "the Create a Research Space model…"

### Parenthetical Citation
Use when the claim, not the author, is the focus.
> Genre analysis has since expanded beyond academic writing (Bhatia, 2004; Hyland, 2006).

### Narrative Citation with Multiple Authors
> Recent work in corpus pragmatics (e.g., Ädel, 2006; McEnery & Hardie, 2012) has…

### Block Quotes
Use when:
- The exact wording matters (definitions, policy text, literary analysis)
- The passage exceeds 40 words (APA) or 4 lines (MLA)

Format:
> [Indent entire block. No quotation marks. Parenthetical citation after the final period.]
>
> (Author, Year, p. XX)

Rules:
- Block quotes are evidence, not argument. Always follow with analysis.
- Do not use block quotes to avoid paraphrasing. If you can paraphrase, paraphrase.

### Citation Density Guidelines
- Literature review: ~1–2 citations per sentence average is normal.
- Results section: cite only when comparing with prior findings.
- Methodology: cite methodological sources for justification.
- Discussion: cite to connect, not to review literature again.
- Never cite without purpose: each citation must support a specific claim.

### Incomplete Citation Patterns to Avoid
- "Several studies have shown…" without naming any → either name them or delete the claim.
- "It is widely accepted that…" → cite or qualify: "In the [field] literature, X is generally treated as…"

## 7. Transition Strategies

### Avoid Formulaic Transitions
Do not use "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Additionally," "In addition" as paragraph openers unless absolutely necessary. These are content-empty — they signal continuation without explaining the logical relationship.

### Content-Based Transitions Instead

| Logical Move | Weak (Formulaic) | Strong (Content-Based) |
|---|---|---|
| Building on prior point | Furthermore, | This limitation becomes more consequential when… |
| Contrast | However, | Where Smith emphasizes structural constraints, Jones foregrounds… |
| Concession | Nevertheless, | Even accounting for sampling bias, the effect size remains… |
| Consequence | Therefore, | If regulatory ambiguity drives this behavior, then… |
| Sequence | Next, | The second design choice — data granularity — introduces… |
| Example | For example, | The Nordic welfare states illustrate this trade-off: … |

### Principle
Every transition should tell the reader the **logical relationship** between the current and prior paragraph, not merely that one follows the other.

### Inter-Section Transitions
At the end of a section or beginning of the next, provide a one-sentence bridge:
> Having established the theoretical framework, the analysis now turns to how these concepts manifest in the empirical data.

## 8. Genre-Specific Conventions

### 8a. Abstract Writing

**Structured abstract** (common in empirical/science journals):
> **Background:** [1–2 sentences of context]
> **Objective:** [1 sentence stating the research question]
> **Methods:** [2–3 sentences: design, participants, analysis]
> **Results:** [2–3 key findings with specifics]
> **Conclusions:** [1–2 sentences: implications, not repetition]

**Unstructured abstract** (common in humanities/social science):
> Single paragraph, 150–250 words, covering: context → gap → method → key findings → significance. Write it last.

**Rules for both:**
- No citations in abstracts (unless journal requires)
- No undefined abbreviations
- No hedging in the abstract that you do not use in the body — or vice versa
- Specific numbers: "reduced latency by 23%" not "significantly reduced latency"

### 8b. Introduction Funnel

```
Broad context     →  "Digital transformation is reshaping public sector service delivery."
Narrower context  →  "Within this shift, interoperability between legacy and cloud systems remains a persistent obstacle."
Specific gap      →  "While architecture-level migration patterns have been catalogued (refs), micro-level data-flow adaptation strategies have received little empirical attention."
Research question →  "This study asks: How do public agencies restructure data pipelines during incremental migration, and what organizational factors predict successful adaptation?"
Contribution      →  "We address this gap through a multi-case study of three municipal agencies, contributing an empirically grounded taxonomy of data-flow adaptation strategies."
Roadmap           →  "Section 2 reviews… Section 3 describes… Section 4 presents…"
```

### 8c. Methodology Justification

Every methodological choice must answer two questions:
1. **What** did you do? (description)
2. **Why** this and not an alternative? (justification)

Template:
> "We employed [method] because [rationale]. Alternative approaches such as [alt1] and [alt2] were considered but rejected on the grounds that [reason]. This design is consistent with [theoretical/epistemological alignment] and has been used in comparable studies (refs)."

### 8d. Results Reporting

- Report findings in order of research questions or hypotheses
- Use tables and figures; reference them in text ("Table 3 shows…")
- Report effect sizes and confidence intervals, not just p-values
- Do not interpret here — save interpretation for Discussion
- Use past tense for completed analyses
- Example: "The mean difference was 4.3 points (SD = 1.2, 95% CI [3.1, 5.5], t(87) = 6.2, p < .001, d = 0.64)."

### 8e. Discussion Structure

Follow the interpret → connect → limit → future pattern:

1. **Interpret**: What do the findings mean? Restate key results in substantive language.
2. **Connect**: How do these results relate to prior work? Confirm, extend, contradict?
3. **Limit**: What constraints affect interpretation? (sample, method, scope, generalizability)
4. **Future**: What should researchers do next? What should practitioners do now?

Template per finding:
> "The observed [finding] suggests that [interpretation]. This aligns with [prior work], which found [related result], but extends it by [novel contribution]. However, [limitation] constrains the generalizability of this claim. Future work should [specific next step]."

## 9. Common Pitfalls

### Vague Pronoun References
Before: "When the model was compared with the baseline, it performed better."
After: "The model outperformed the baseline on recall (0.91 vs. 0.78)."

**Rule:** Every "it," "this," "they" must have an unambiguous antecedent. When in doubt, repeat the noun.

### Passive Voice Overuse
Before: "It was found that the algorithm was outperformed by the baseline."
After: "The baseline outperformed the algorithm."

**Guideline:** Passive is appropriate when the agent is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately backgrounded. It is not a default. Use active voice unless passive serves a specific rhetorical purpose.

### Nominalization
Before: "The facilitation of the improvement of student outcomes was accomplished through the implementation of peer review."
After: "Peer review improved student outcomes."

**Rule:** If you can convert a nominalization to its verb form and the sentence becomes shorter and clearer, do it.

### Hedge Stacking
Before: "It might perhaps possibly suggest a potential tendency toward improvement."
After: "These results suggest a modest improvement."

**Rule:** One hedge per claim, calibrated to evidence strength (see §4).

### Claiming Too Much from Data
Before: "The data prove that the intervention works across all contexts."
After: "In this sample, the intervention produced statistically significant gains in [outcome]. Replication in other contexts is necessary."

**Rule:** Data support claims within the bounds of the study design. Extrapolation requires explicit qualification.

### Other Pitfalls
- **Straw-man literature reviews:** Do not weaken prior work to strengthen yours. Represent opposing positions at their strongest.
- **Methodology as recipe:** Do not copy-paste method sections across papers without adapting to each study's specific design.
- **Results–Discussion bleed:** If you find yourself interpreting in the Results section, move that sentence to Discussion.
- **Orphan citations:** A citation with no analytical purpose is dead weight. Remove it or give it work to do.

## 10. Revision Checklist

Use this checklist after completing a draft. Check each item; if it fails, revise before considering the draft done.

### Argument Coherence
- [ ] Does every section serve the central claim?
- [ ] Can a reader state the thesis argument after reading only the introduction and conclusion?
- [ ] Are there sections that feel tangential? If so, integrate them or cut them.
- [ ] Does each sub-argument in the Discussion resolve or advance the main claim?

### Evidence–Citation Alignment
- [ ] Is every factual claim supported by a citation or your own data?
- [ ] Are there citations with no corresponding claim (orphan citations)?
- [ ] Do block quotes receive analysis after quotation?
- [ ] Are hedging levels appropriate to the evidence strength for each claim?

### Register Consistency
- [ ] Is the formality level consistent throughout (no sudden colloquialisms)?
- [ ] Are technical terms used consistently (no synonym-switching for variety)?
- [ ] Is person (first/third) consistent with discipline norms?
- [ ] Are tense choices consistent (past for methods/results, present for general truths)?

### Paragraph Variation
- [ ] Do paragraph lengths vary (3–12 sentences)?
- [ ] Do paragraph openings vary (no three consecutive paragraphs starting the same way)?
- [ ] Does every paragraph advance the argument (not merely summarize a source)?
- [ ] Are there paragraphs that could be split or merged?

### Transition Quality
- [ ] Have all formulaic transitions (Moreover, Furthermore, Additionally) been replaced with content-based alternatives?
- [ ] Does each paragraph opening tell the reader its logical relationship to the previous paragraph?
- [ ] Are inter-section transitions present and informative?

### Final Passes
- [ ] Read aloud to catch rhythm problems (sentences that stall, paragraphs that drag).
- [ ] Check that the abstract matches the body (no phantom claims).
- [ ] Verify all citations appear in the reference list and vice versa.
- [ ] Remove any sentence that does no work. If removing it changes nothing, it should not be there.
